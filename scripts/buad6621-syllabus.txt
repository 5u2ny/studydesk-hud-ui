COURSE DESCRIPTION
This course will focus on the concepts and tools related to the management of projects within organizations. Students will examine all phases of project management, including selection, planning, scheduling, control, and termination. Topics include writing project plans, developing work breakdown structures, project scheduling, project management tools, resource management, earned value analysis, and project risk management.
LEARNING GOALS AND OBJECTIVES
Upon successful completion of this course, you will be able to:
Identify and explain project management resources, issues, and best practices.
Demonstrate a project management tool to fellow classmates.
Develop a project charter.
Create and present a project management plan.
Produce a project schedule using a project management tool.
Apply project management knowledge through a project management simulation and project reflection.
Leverage AI strategically for insights, prediction, and ethical delivery.

PROJECT MANAGEMENT SIMULATION GAME (SIM 1 & SIM 2)
An essential part of this course is to apply the project management knowledge you have gained in the course to a simulated model (Scenario A). This will allow you to test and reflect on your understanding and comprehension of the course material in a seemingly real environment. The SIM1 assignment is a group/team assignment (CATEGORY B, Level 3). Each team will turn in a memo describing their approach to the project, their results, and the lessons they learned that could be applied to real-world projects.
NOTE: In SIM 2, you will be doing an individual attempt on another version of the simulation, so do not rely on just one team member purchasing it.
The SIM2 assignment will allow you to test your individual understanding and comprehension of the course material in another safe yet seemingly real environment (Scenario B). This assignment is an individual assignment (CATEGORY A, Level 2).
PROJECT CHARTER (ProjCharter) & MANAGEMENT PLAN (PMP) PROJECT
Details of the Project Charter (ProjCharter) and the final project management plan deliverable (PMP) are available on Blackboard. The ProjCharter and PMP are group/team assignments (CATEGORY B, Level 4). A project manager creates a brief document called the project charter. It establishes the project's foundation and authorizes the use of resources. In the Team Evaluation assignment, you will evaluate all team members (including yourself) on each team member’s value-add to the final product. Leadership and effort will be viewed favorably, while the lack of adequate participation will adversely impact your grade.

Students are expected to adhere to the College of William and Mary Honor Code and to the general principles of academic honesty. Within the Mason School of Business, we have developed an Assignment Code and every assignment given in this course will be identified by a code letter. If there are questions related to how an assignment is to be carried out, it is essential that you ask your professor for clarification.
The code is as follows:
CATEGORY A: This is an individual assignment. You may not receive help from anyone on this assignment. It must be 100% your own work. All questions concerning this assignment should be addressed to your professor. It is an honor code offense to give or receive assistance on this assignment.
CATEGORY B: This is a group assignment. Your group may not receive help from anyone outside your group. While your group may choose to delegate the work among the group members, everyone in the group is expected to be prepared to discuss the entire assignment in class. All questions concerning this assignment should be addressed to your professor. It is an honor code offense to give help to other groups and individuals or receive assistance from other groups and individuals.
CATEGORY C: This is an individual assignment. You may work with others or receive help from a tutor on this assignment. You must, however, turn in your own paper. You may not divide the work with others or copy another student’s paper; it is an honor code offense to do so.
CATEGORY D: This is a group assignment. You may share information, discuss general concepts and approaches to the assignment with other groups. Everyone in the group should be prepared to discuss the entire assignment in class. Each group must turn in its own work. You may not copy another group’s work; it is an honor code offense to do so.
CATEGORY E: This is a timed assignment. You are given a specific length of time within which the work must be completed. It is an honor code offense to violate this time restriction unless you have received permission from your professor.

Developments in AI technology are advancing rapidly. Of note, many software platforms (e.g., Microsoft Office Suite) are expected to embed AI soon. To help guide you in your use of AI, I have provided the following categories:
LEVEL 1 – No AI Assistance. You may not receive help from any AI tools, including editing applications (e.g., Grammarly), generative AI (e.g., ChatGPT), or AI tools integrated within software platforms (e.g., Microsoft Office). LEVEL 2 –  AI for Learning and Practice. You may use AI tools for learning and practice. It is an honor code offense to use AI tools for more
than brainstorming without explicit permission.
LEVEL 4 – AI for Limited or Specific Tasks. You may use AI tools as outlined by your instructor in the assignment guidelines. You must clearly indicate in your citations when and how you used AI for these purposes, and you cannot use AI to generate or assist in generating content beyond that outlined in the assignment. It is an honor code offense to use AI tools for more than the strictly outlined tasks without explicit permission.

PLAGIARISM:
The Internet provides vast resources for students to research topics and develop their personal knowledge. As a writer at the university level, we expect you to synthesize existing information and create your own argument. Merely copying the words of others is not sufficient, nor is stringing together a series of statements and information created by others. I encourage you to review the information provided by the Writing & Communication Center (https://www.wm.edu/as/wcc) and to consult with their consultants.
The pursuit of knowledge often involves referring to other sources and building upon the prior work of others. We expect students to acknowledge these sources in the form of citation and, if using the words or phrases of others, direct quotation. A reader/reviewer should be able to discern what portion of your paper represents
your original thought and argument. Plagiarism is “the presentation, with intent to deceive, or with disregard for proper scholarly procedures of a significant scope, of any information, ideas or phrasing of another as if they were one’s own without giving appropriate credit to the original source.”
Proper scholarly procedures require you to organize your writing such that you keep track of original source material so you can cite it appropriately in your final paper. I recommend you develop a system to do so from the beginning of the course, and I would be happy to provide you with suggestions as to how to do so.
